1. M. de Azcarate's visit to Greece.
M. de Azcarate said that he had been received with the greatest cordiality in Greece. He had been particularly struck by the Refugee Colonies he had visited, by the courage of the refugees themselves, and by the manner in which the colonies were administered.
£. Opium Limitation Conference.
Mr. Ekstrand said that he was satisfied with the progress cf the Conference. Different methods for the limitation of the manufacture of drugs had been proposed by various countries, but negotiations were afoot to reach a common solution. All the leading Delegations were determined not to leave the Conference without attaining concrete results.
In reply to the Secretary-General, Mr. Ekstrand stated that he thought the Conference would probably last about a fortnight longer.
3.	M. Aghnides drew attention to the connunication iron the United States of America regarding the state of their armaments. This conmunication followed the form approved by the Council during its recent session, and it was remarkable that the United States Government had been able to furnish the material in that form at such short notice. Already it appeared that Japan was likely to follow suit in sending early information.
4.	Ur. Buxton said that for the present and for a few weeks longer it was necessary to resort to the services of amateur interpreters, and he hoped that Directors would not be too critical of mistakes due to lack of experience.
5.	Economy.
The Secretary-General drew attention to the serious financial situation. Economy was essential, for he did not know where to turn for more money; Sections, like Governments, were, he knew, all in favour of economy in principle, but they often seemed against it in practice when it applied tc themselves. He appealed to everyone to take his proposal for economy very seriously indeed. Deductions in selaries had teen made in many Government services, and he felt that the only way to avoid a general reduction in Secretariat salaries was to show that economies could be and were being made in other ways. Such a general reduction, if it occurred, would fall very heavily on the lower paid members of the staff.

(a)	Différend sino-japonais*
Le SECRETAIRE GENERAL expose que le Conseil vient d’avoir une longue et difficile session à Paris. Il a pu sembler à un moment donné que la situation était sans issue; nais finalement, on a pu aboutir au texte d’un projet de résolution qui a été accepté à l’unanimité par le Conseil. On peut dire que la Société des Nations est sortie dans les meilleures conditions possibles d’une situation très délicate. Si la session a duré si longtemps, cela est dû au fait que, non seulement chaque mot de la résolution et de la déclaration du Président était passé au crible par les représentants

Les résultats de la session de Paris font l’objet de critiques; ils présentent néanmoins un caractère réconfortant en ce sens que les Parties - ou tout au moins les Chinois et Japonais raisonnables- prétendent avoir obtenu un succès. Cela est vrai dans un certain sens. Si la Chine n’a pas obtenu l’évacuation immédiate des régions occupées par les Japonais en dehors de la zone du chemin de fer en Mandchourie, elle a obtenu que son différend avec le Japon soit porté sur le plan international et qu’une commission d’étude se rendre sur place. Le Japon par contre, s’il n’a pu obliger la Chine à entrer en pourparlers directs avec lui et à faire considérer le conflit en Mandchourie comme intéressant seules les deux Parties en cause, a obtenu qu’aucun délai ne soit fixé pour l’évacuation et que le mandat de la Commission soit des plus larges, celle-ci étant chargée de faire rapport au Conseil su. toute circonstance de caractère à affecter les relations internationales menaçant de troubler la paix entre la Chine et le Japon.
Les critiques formulées contre le Conseil pour ne pas avoir obtenu l’évacuation immédiate de la Mandchourie sont injustes, car elles ne tiennent pas compte de la situation spéciale existant dans cette région.
La Commission d'étude devra être composée de personnages de tout premier ordre, car sa tâche sera longue et difficile et les conclusions auxquelles elle aboutira pourront être d’une grande importance en vue de régler une série de questions qui se posent en Lxtrême-Orient.

Council Procedure - Note for the President.
On the proposal of the British Representative, we adopted as an experiment at the January session a modification in our procedure for reading rapporteurs’ reports.
I think it was generally agreed that that experiment was a success and economised time, without detracting from the value or the interest of our proceedings,
I therefore propose that we adopt it for this and future Council sessions.
There is one point in the procedure to which I should like to call attention.	When a report is not read "the
rapporteur should make such explanations or amplifications of it as he considers necessary". In more or less routine business, the rapporteur will probably feel that the written report is sufficient, but in matters of greater importance, I think it would help the discussion if the rapporteur would in a few words bring out the main points of his report.
I am a little doubtful as to the second paragraph, but I think chis might well be considered at the Directors meeting to-morrow afternoon,, The Information Section will be specially interested.
