1.	Slno-Jananese dispute.
The SECHETaHY-GENERAL, while admitting that conditions had improved in the Shanghai area, was not sure that much progress had teen made in regard to Manchuria since the beginning of the special session of the Assembly. The Committee of Nineteen had recognised that it had to deal with the dispute as a whole but was prepared to deal with the two aspects separately, i.e. Shanghai before Manchuria.
The question had arisen whether the Commission of Inquiry was to report to the Assembly direct, or to the Council which had created it. The Commissi on of Nineteen had agreed that any suggestions from the Committee of Inquiry should reach it through the Council.
The Chinese and Japanese Governments had been asked to what extent they had executed or intended to execute the Council’s resolutions of September 30 and December 10. Much depended on the answer given to this question. If the Japanese Government maintained the reservation which they had made to the effect that the assembly was not
competent to deal with the Manchurian aspect of the problem it might be necessary to refer the point to the Permanent Court. In a statement made before the Japanese Diet on the previous day M.Yoshizawa seemed to indicate that the reservation would be maintained. The Secretary-General personally was of opinion that this was not altogether an easy position to defend as though the Japanese delegate had abstains d from the vote on the assembly's resolution of March 11 he had not opposed it so that this resolution, by which the Assembly declared itself seized of the whole question (i.e. in its Manchurian as in its other aspects), had full force.
2.	Pisarmament.
The SECHPfAMT-GMMMrb-xL said that the expectation that the general discussion 'would be finished before Easter had not been fulfilled, as work of the Conference had been impeded to some extent by the special session of the Assembly. Therefore when the Conference met again on April 11 it would be faced with a discussion of the questions of principle which arose. This would certainly be difficult but he derived some hope from the fact that already on one or two points on which before the Conference no agreement had seemed possible there already seemed now to be a substantial measure of agreement.
M.aGIIMIDES said that one of the initial difficulties of the Conference had been that there was no agenda. The acceptance of Sir John Simon's proposal that the Draft Convention of the Preparatory Committee should be taken as a framework for the proceedings of the Conference, each delegation being free to propose any amendments, additions
3
or suppressions, had enabled the Conference to surmount this initial difficulty.
The five Conference commissions the constitution of which had been foreseen had in fact been set up and in addition to them a sixth, the Political Commission.
Though it had been doubted at first whether the Political Commission was necessary it now seemed clear that it would serve a useful purpose.
The work of the technical commissions had to some extent been impeded by the fact that questions of principle on the solution of which their further progress depended had not been solved. In April the General Commission would be called upon to settle immediately these questions of principle, at any rate to that extent which was necessary in order to enable the technical committees to proceed.
The work of the Conference had also been delayed or embarrassed by the Sino-Japanese dispute, the economic crisis the prospect of elections and the reparations problem, but in spite of these difficulties he thought that a certain amount of optimism as to its results was justified.
The SECRLfAHY-GHNERaL agreed. The opening procedure of the Conference had been slow for reasons which were not within the control of delegates. His own feeling was that though the Conference might not achieve as much as was generally hoped this was a matter in which the beginning was half the work since a first convention would be revised from time to time. Such a revision of the decisions of the Conference which was required by the Covenant every ten years should he thought be undertaken every five years.
3.	Financial Committee.
Mr.FELKIN said that the Financial Committee was expected to conclude on the following day a long and anxious

session. It had considered, in addition to the requests of Austria, Hungary, Greece and Bulgaria, certain general problems such as the restoration of credit and of foreign lending. The report should be available soon after '¿aster. It was not yet certain whether it would be made public before it had been discussed by the Council.
4.	Budget.
The SECRüTAh f-	thanked the Directors for the
way in which they had assisted him in preparing the draft budget for 1933. He felt that he could submit this draft with confidence to the Supervisory Commission, as, apart from certain special charges, it would not materially exceed the budget for 1931, which, in view of the increase in the Lea-rue's work, really represented a considerable saving.
He would remind Directors that his frequent appeals for economy, troublesome though they might be, continued to be necessary. It was only an unexpected payment which had enabled the Treasury recently to pay salaries out of the cash balance.
Mr.SWELTSHR pointed out that the Congress of the United States had voted a certain sum representing the contribution of the U.8.A. to the Disarmament Co: ill erenoe and it was agreed that steps should be taken to receive -this money within the present year.
